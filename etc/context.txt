TRUTH AND FALSEHOOD
The definition of truth and falsehood, which is our topic to-day, lies strictly outside our general subject, namely the analysis of mind.
The purely formal definition of truth and falsehood offers little difficulty.
In this case the correspondence which constitutes truth is very simple.
In other words, the question of what constitutes truth is identical with the question of the distinction between truth and error.
Truth exists in the form of natural phenomena, and whatever is, is true.
The universe is the truth.
Every thing, every sense perception, no matter how subjective or shortlived it may be, is true, is a certain part of truth.
Truth must be objective, that is to say it must be the truth about some concrete object.
Truths are valid only under certain conditions, and under certain conditions errors may be true.
Truth is that which is common or general to our reasoning faculty within a given circle of sense perceptions.
For truth is so great that it comprises absolutely everything.
So you may have some difficulty in distinguishing between concepts of truth, and true concepts.
It is the same with truth and untruth.
Our logic is therefore the science of truth.
The latter does not produce truth, but only the understanding of truth.
Logical truth must inevitably be a part of absolute truth, and it is our duty to remove absolute truth from the field of metaphysics, of transcendentalism, and to transfer it to the sober world which forms an inseparable unit with the human mind.
Truth is here and truth is there.
Truth is so divine that it is everywhere and absolute.
Truth is the absolute, universal sum of all existing things, of all phenomena of the past, present, and future.
Truth is the real universe from which errors and lies are not excluded.
The problem of truth is a problem of philosophy.
